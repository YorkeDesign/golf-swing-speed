## Executive Summary

Audio analysis presents a **moderately promising supplementary channel** for golf swing speed measurement, though it is unlikely to serve as a standalone speed measurement technique with useful accuracy. The strongest use cases are:

1. **Precise impact moment detection** -- The sharp acoustic spike at ball contact can pinpoint impact timing to within 1-2 ms, providing a reliable trigger/timestamp for camera-based analysis.
2. **Swing phase segmentation** -- The transition from silence to whoosh to impact crack provides clear audio landmarks for segmenting the swing.
3. **Qualitative speed correlation** -- Louder, higher-pitched whoosh sounds correlate with faster swings, but converting this to a precise mph figure is unreliable without calibration.
4. **Shot quality assessment** -- Impact sound spectral characteristics correlate with strike quality (center vs. off-center hits).

Direct speed measurement from audio Doppler shift or aeroacoustic frequency analysis is **theoretically possible but practically very difficult** on a consumer iPhone due to environmental noise, variable geometry, and the complexity of the acoustic signal.

## The Physics of Golf Swing Sound

### Aeroacoustic Noise Generation (The "Whoosh")

The whoosh sound of a golf club swing is generated by two primary mechanisms:

**1. Turbulent Wake Noise**
As the club moves through air, it creates turbulent pressure fluctuations in its wake. This generates broadband noise (a wide range of frequencies) whose intensity scales with velocity. The faster the motion, the stronger and higher-frequency the pressure fluctuations, producing a louder and often higher-pitched sound.

**2. Vortex Shedding (Aeolian Tones)**
When air flows past the club shaft (a roughly cylindrical body), vortices are shed alternately from each side at a frequency governed by the Strouhal number relationship:

```
f = (St x V) / D

Where:
  f  = vortex shedding frequency (Hz)
  St = Strouhal number (~0.2 for cylinders at relevant Reynolds numbers)
  V  = flow velocity (m/s)
  D  = characteristic diameter (m)
```

### Calculated Frequencies for Golf Club Shaft

Golf club shafts have a tip diameter of approximately 9.0-9.4 mm (0.355"-0.370") and butt diameter of approximately 15.2 mm (0.600"). Using the Strouhal formula:

| Speed (mph) | Speed (m/s) | Shaft Tip (~9mm) | Shaft Butt (~15mm) |
|-------------|-------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| 60          | 26.8        | ~596 Hz           | ~357 Hz             |
| 80          | 35.8        | ~796 Hz           | ~477 Hz             |
| 100         | 44.7        | ~993 Hz           | ~596 Hz             |
| 120         | 53.6        | ~1191 Hz          | ~715 Hz             |

These frequencies fall well within the audible range (20 Hz - 20 kHz) and within iPhone microphone capture capabilities. **Critically, the frequency is linearly proportional to velocity**, meaning that if you could isolate the vortex shedding tone from the broadband noise, you could theoretically extract speed.

### Practical Complications

- The club is **not a uniform cylinder** -- it has a large, irregularly shaped clubhead at one end.
- The clubhead generates its own complex turbulent wake and pressure field.
- The shaft **flexes and vibrates** during the swing, adding structural vibration tones.
- The signal is a **mixture** of broadband turbulent noise, tonal vortex shedding, and structural vibration -- separating these components is non-trivial.
- **Environmental noise** (wind, other golfers, traffic) contaminates the signal.
- The club velocity **changes rapidly** through the swing arc, meaning frequencies are sweeping, not steady-state.

## Sound-Based Speed Detection Methods

### Method 1: Doppler Effect Analysis

**Concept:** A moving sound source exhibits a frequency shift relative to a stationary observer. If the club generates a characteristic tone, the observed frequency will be higher during approach and lower during recession.

**Doppler Formula:**
```
f' = f x (c / (c - v))

Where:
  f' = observed frequency
  f  = emitted frequency
  c  = speed of sound (~343 m/s)
  v  = source velocity (positive = approaching)
```

**For a club at 100 mph (44.7 m/s):**
```
Approach:  f' = f x (343 / (343 - 44.7)) = f x 1.150  (+15.0% shift)
Recession: f' = f x (343 / (343 + 44.7)) = f x 0.885  (-11.5% shift)
```

A 15% frequency shift is significant and theoretically detectable. However:

- The club is **not a pure tone source** -- it emits broadband noise, making frequency tracking difficult.
- The vortex shedding frequency itself changes with speed (it is speed-dependent), so you get a **compound effect**: the emitted frequency is increasing AND the Doppler shift is increasing simultaneously.
- The phone is stationary but the **club passes by at close range**, creating near-field effects.
- The angular relationship between club path and microphone changes throughout the swing.

**Feasibility: LOW** for direct speed measurement. The compounding of speed-dependent emission frequency with Doppler shift makes deconvolution extremely challenging without a known reference tone.

### Method 2: Aeroacoustic Frequency Tracking

**Concept:** Track the vortex shedding frequency of the shaft in real-time using Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) or wavelet analysis. Since f = (St x V) / D, extracting f gives V.

**Approach:**
1. Record audio at 48 kHz during swing.
2. Apply STFT with short windows (5-10 ms) to get time-frequency representation.
3. Look for a frequency peak that sweeps upward during downswing.
4. Convert detected frequency to speed using known shaft diameter and Strouhal number.

**Challenges:**
- The tonal component may be **weak relative to broadband turbulent noise**, especially from the clubhead.
- Short analysis windows reduce frequency resolution (uncertainty principle: delta-f x delta-t >= 1).
- A 5ms window at 48 kHz = 240 samples, giving frequency resolution of ~200 Hz -- too coarse for useful speed discrimination.
- A 20ms window gives ~50 Hz resolution but the club speed changes significantly in 20ms near impact.

**Feasibility: LOW-MEDIUM** for absolute speed. Could potentially distinguish broad speed categories (slow/medium/fast) but unlikely to achieve better than +/-15 mph accuracy.

### Method 3: Sound Intensity / Envelope Analysis

**Concept:** The overall loudness (sound pressure level) of the swing whoosh increases with speed. Aeroacoustic noise power scales roughly with V^6 to V^8 for turbulent flow past bluff bodies.

**Approach:**
1. Record audio during swing.
2. Compute the RMS amplitude envelope over time.
3. Calibrate the peak amplitude against known speeds.

**Challenges:**
- Highly dependent on **microphone distance and orientation** to the club.
- Phone placement varies between sessions and users.
- Environmental noise floor varies.
- The V^6 scaling means small loudness differences correspond to large speed differences at high speeds, which is actually favorable for sensitivity, but **absolute calibration is impractical** without a controlled reference.

**Feasibility: LOW** for absolute measurement. MEDIUM for relative comparison between swings in the same session if the phone position is consistent.

## Impact Sound Analysis

### Acoustic Characteristics of Golf Ball Impact

According to PubMed, Shannon & Axe (2002) conducted foundational research on the acoustic signature of golf ball impact, measuring and analyzing the sound produced by sharp impact loading of a golf ball. [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1080/026404102320183194)

Key findings from the body of research on golf impact acoustics:

- Impact sounds are **short in duration** (a few milliseconds) and contain a **broad range of frequencies**, mainly between 1 and 10 kHz.
- Instantaneous sound pressure levels at the ear can reach peaks of **up to 127 dB**.
- The acoustic response of a club has been identified as a **key factor in how a player perceives the "feel" of a shot**.
- Stronger correlations were found with sound data than vibration data, suggesting sound is the most significant form of feedback from impact.
- Frequency content in the **2-4 kHz range** correlates with ball compression values.
- Below 2 kHz, frequency content depends more on **putter/club type and impact location** than on ball construction.
- Positive correlations exist between subjective shot quality ratings and acoustic metrics including Sound Pressure Level, Zwicker's loudness, and sharpness.

### What Impact Sound Can Tell Us

| Metric | Relationship to Impact Sound | Reliability |
|--------|------------------------------|-------------|
| Strike quality (center/off-center) | Frequency spectrum and sharpness change | HIGH |
| Club type identification | Different clubs have distinct spectral signatures | HIGH |
| Ball compression | Peaks in 2-4 kHz range correlate | MEDIUM |
| Clubhead speed | Louder impact at higher speeds, but many confounds | LOW |
| Impact timing | Sharp onset provides precise timestamp | VERY HIGH |

### Impact Moment Detection

The most valuable application of impact sound analysis is **precisely detecting the moment of impact**. The ball strike produces a sharp transient signal (essentially a pulse) that rises from background noise to peak amplitude in under 1 millisecond. This creates an unambiguous temporal marker that can be detected with simple threshold-based algorithms.

**Detection algorithm:**
```
1. Compute running RMS of audio signal in 1ms windows
2. Calculate derivative of RMS envelope
3. Detect when derivative exceeds threshold (sharp onset)
4. Timestamp = detected onset time
```

This is far more precise than trying to detect impact from video frames alone (where impact occurs between frames at typical recording rates).

## iPhone Microphone Capabilities

### Hardware Specifications

Based on measurements by Faber Acoustical for recent iPhone models (15 Pro Max, 16 Pro, 17 Pro):

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Native sample rate | 48 kHz |
| Nyquist frequency | 24 kHz |
| Usable frequency range | ~20 Hz to ~20 kHz |
| Number of microphones | 3 (bottom, front, back) |
| Bit depth | 24-bit (via Core Audio) |
| Maximum SPL handling | ~120-130 dB SPL (varies by model) |
| Low-frequency response | De-emphasized below ~100 Hz |

### Key Considerations

- **Sample rate:** 48 kHz is adequate for all golf-relevant frequencies (vortex shedding up to ~1.2 kHz, impact sounds up to ~10 kHz).
- **Directional sensitivity:** The bottom microphone has directional bias -- sound from the microphone side is better received, especially at higher frequencies where wavelengths are shorter than the phone body.
- **SPL handling:** Golf impacts can reach 127 dB at the ear. At the typical phone distance (3-6 feet from impact), levels will be lower but may still approach or exceed the microphone's maximum SPL, causing clipping. This needs testing.
- **Wind noise:** Outdoor use introduces wind noise that overlaps with low-frequency swing sounds. A high-pass filter above 200-300 Hz would be needed.

Sources: [iPhone Microphone Frequency Response Comparison - Faber Acoustical](https://blog.faberacoustical.com/wpblog/2009/ios/iphone/iphone-microphone-frequency-response-comparison/), [iPhone 16 Pro Microphone Measurements](https://blog.faberacoustical.com/wpblog/2024/ios/iphone/measured-iphone-16-pro-microphone-frequency-response-and-directivity/), [iPhone 17 Pro Microphone Measurements](https://blog.faberacoustical.com/wpblog/2025/ios/iphone/measured-iphone-17-pro-microphone-frequency-response-and-directivity/)

## iOS Audio Processing Framework

### AVAudioEngine

Apple's `AVAudioEngine` provides a real-time audio processing graph with:

- **Buffer capture:** `installTap(onBus:)` delivers audio buffers at configurable intervals (as low as ~5ms).
- **Low latency:** Using the `.measurement` audio session category minimizes processing latency. Default mode adds ~30ms due to echo cancellation; measurement mode reduces round-trip to ~5-10ms.
- **Sample-accurate timing:** Buffer timestamps are sample-accurate relative to the audio clock.

### Accelerate / vDSP Framework

Apple's `vDSP` (part of the Accelerate framework) provides hardware-optimized DSP:

- **FFT:** `vDSP_fft_zrip` for real-to-complex FFT, essential for frequency analysis.
- **Windowing:** Hanning, Hamming, Blackman windows for spectral analysis.
- **Filtering:** FIR and IIR filter design and application.
- **Performance:** Leverages NEON SIMD on Apple Silicon, can process 4096-point FFTs in microseconds.

### Practical Implementation Path

```
Audio Session (.measurement mode, 48 kHz)
    |
AVAudioEngine (installTap, 1024-sample buffers = 21ms)
    |
    +-- RMS Envelope Detector (impact detection)
    |       -> Threshold crossing = impact timestamp
    |
    +-- High-pass Filter (>200 Hz, remove wind noise)
    |       |
    |       +-- STFT (512-point, 50% overlap = ~5ms hop)
    |               -> Spectrogram for frequency tracking
    |
    +-- Peak Amplitude Tracker
            -> Swing intensity metric
```

**Latency budget:** For supplementary timing data, 5-10ms latency is acceptable. The audio timestamp can be correlated with video frame timestamps retroactively (not in real-time).

Sources: [AVAudioEngine Documentation](https://developer.apple.com/documentation/avfaudio/avaudioengine), [Superpowered Latency Test](https://superpowered.com/latency), [Overloud iOS Latency Guide](https://www.overloud.com/node/325)

## Audio as Supplementary Data

This is where audio analysis has the **highest practical value** -- not as a standalone speed measurement, but as a complementary data channel to camera-based tracking.

### Use Case 1: Impact Moment Detection (HIGH VALUE)

**Problem it solves:** At 240 FPS, each frame spans 4.17ms. The club moves ~7.5 inches per frame at 100 mph. Determining which frame contains the exact impact moment is ambiguous from video alone.

**Audio solution:** The impact sound provides a timestamp accurate to ~1ms. By synchronizing audio and video timelines (both use the same device clock on iPhone), the exact impact frame (and sub-frame position) can be identified.

**Implementation complexity:** LOW. Simple amplitude threshold detection on the audio stream.

### Use Case 2: Swing Phase Segmentation (MEDIUM-HIGH VALUE)

**Problem it solves:** Automatically detecting when the swing starts, the transition from backswing to downswing, and when the follow-through is complete.

**Audio phases of a golf swing:**
1. **Address silence** -- Relatively quiet, ambient noise only.
2. **Backswing** -- Very faint whoosh (slow movement, low noise).
3. **Transition** -- Brief pause in sound intensity at the top of backswing.
4. **Downswing** -- Rapidly increasing whoosh sound, rising in both amplitude and frequency.
5. **Impact** -- Sharp, loud transient spike (10-20 dB above whoosh).
6. **Follow-through** -- Decreasing whoosh sound.
7. **Post-swing silence** -- Return to ambient levels.

**Detection approach:** A state machine driven by audio envelope analysis:
```
WAITING -> (amplitude rises above threshold) -> SWING_ACTIVE
SWING_ACTIVE -> (sharp transient detected) -> IMPACT_DETECTED
IMPACT_DETECTED -> (amplitude falls below threshold) -> SWING_COMPLETE
```

**Implementation complexity:** LOW-MEDIUM. Requires tuning thresholds and possibly a small ML classifier for robust detection across environments.

### Use Case 3: Swing Detection Trigger (HIGH VALUE)

**Problem it solves:** The app needs to know when to start recording high-FPS video. Continuously recording at 240 FPS drains battery and storage.

**Audio solution:** Monitor audio in a low-power mode. When the rising whoosh pattern is detected (amplitude increasing over 200-300ms), trigger high-FPS video recording. The audio monitoring requires far less power than continuous high-FPS video.

**Timing consideration:** There is a ~100-200ms window between when the downswing whoosh becomes detectable and impact. This may be tight for triggering video capture, but if the camera is already in a moderate-FPS standby mode, it could switch to high-FPS in time for the critical impact zone.

### Use Case 4: Calibration Cross-Check (MEDIUM VALUE)

**Problem it solves:** Validating that camera-based speed estimates are reasonable.

**Audio approach:** After collecting a set of swings with both camera speed measurements and audio characteristics, build a regression model:
```
audio_features = [peak_amplitude, spectral_centroid, bandwidth, duration_of_whoosh]
estimated_speed_range = regression_model(audio_features)
```

If the camera-measured speed falls outside the audio-estimated range, flag it as potentially erroneous. This does not require audio to be precise -- even a +/-20 mph estimate is useful as a sanity check.

### Use Case 5: Shot Quality Feedback (MEDIUM VALUE)

**Problem it solves:** Providing feedback on strike quality beyond just speed.

**Audio approach:** Analyze the spectral characteristics of the impact sound:
- Center strike: Brighter, higher-frequency content, sharper onset.
- Toe/heel strike: Duller, more low-frequency energy, longer onset.
- Thin shot: Higher-pitched, "clicky" sound.
- Fat shot: Muffled, preceded by ground-impact sound.

This could complement speed data with qualitative strike feedback.

## Academic Research

### Golf-Specific Acoustic Research

**Shannon & Axe (2002) -- "On the acoustic signature of golf ball impact"**
Journal of Sports Sciences, 20(8), 629-33.
Foundational study establishing methods for measuring and analyzing golf ball impact acoustics. Found that impact sound can reveal ball elastic properties and compression characteristics.
[DOI: 10.1080/026404102320183194](https://doi.org/10.1080/026404102320183194)

**Roberts et al. (2005) -- "Evaluation of impact sound on the 'feel' of a golf shot"**
Published in Journal of Sound and Vibration. Found that sound is more significant than vibration for how players perceive shot quality. Impact sounds contain frequencies mainly in the 1-10 kHz range with peaks up to 127 dB SPL.
Source: [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0022460X04009137)

**Roberts et al. (2005) -- "Assessment of the impact sound in golf putting"**
Found that frequency content below 2 kHz depends on putter type and impact location, while 2-4 kHz content correlates with ball compression.
Source: [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/7170927_Assessment_of_the_impact_sound_in_golf_putting)

**Roberts et al. (2006) -- "Influence of sound and vibration from sports impacts on players' perceptions of equipment quality"**
Extended the work to show positive correlations between subjective quality ratings and acoustic metrics (SPL, Zwicker loudness, sharpness).
Source: [SAGE Journals](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1243/14644207JMDA86)

### Related Sports Acoustic Research

**Russell, D.A. (2017) -- "Acoustics and Vibration of Baseball and Softball Bats"**
Acoustics Today. Comprehensive analysis of bat vibration modes and acoustic signatures. Found that ball-on-bat impact radiates sound at 8.5-12 kHz from ball deformation (spherical shell buckling). Bending mode frequencies vary by bat construction.
Source: [Acoustics Today PDF](https://acousticstoday.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/12/Acoustics-and-Vibration-of-Baseball-and-Softball-Bats-Daniel-A.-Russell.pdf)

**Cricket bat acoustic analysis (Magnus Cricket, 2025)**
Analysis of cricket bat sounds showing that bat speed, angle, and ball position affect sound characteristics. Well-timed shots produce sharper, louder sounds; poorly timed shots sound flatter. Off-center hits produce lower-pitched or muffled sounds.
Source: [Magnus Cricket](https://www.magnuscricket.com/blogs/news/the-science-of-cricket-sound-what-the-noise-off-the-bat-really-tells-you)

### Audio Signal Processing Research

**Sound Event Detection with Deep Learning**
Convolutional recurrent neural networks (CRNN) are state-of-the-art for sound event detection. Features are extracted as mel-spectrograms. Some approaches apply object-detection techniques (e.g., Mask R-CNN) to spectrograms to identify event boundaries.
Sources: [Sound Event Detection - Jonnor](https://jonnor.github.io/machinehearing/europython2021/slides.html), [MDPI - Ensemble CNN for Sound Event Detection](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/13/9/1502)

### Smartphone Doppler Research

**FizziQ / Doppler Effect Experiments with Smartphones**
Demonstrated that smartphone microphones (at 44-48 kHz sample rate) can detect Doppler frequency shifts from moving vehicles. The technique works well for objects emitting steady tones but is much harder for broadband noise sources.
Source: [FizziQ Doppler Experiments](https://www.fizziq.org/en/post/experiment-doppler-effect-1)

**Rapp, D. -- "Motion sensing using the Doppler effect"**
Web demonstration showing that a speaker/microphone pair on a computer can detect motion via Doppler shift of an emitted tone. This active sonar approach is more feasible than passive listening.
Source: [danielrapp.github.io/doppler](https://danielrapp.github.io/doppler/)

## Multi-Modal Audio-Video Fusion

### Concept

Rather than relying on audio alone, combine audio and video streams for improved measurement:

```
Video Stream (240 FPS)          Audio Stream (48 kHz)
        |                               |
  Club tracking                  Envelope detection
  Speed estimation               Impact timestamp
        |                               |
        +------- Fusion Layer ----------+
                     |
              Refined estimate:
              - Video speed at audio-determined impact time
              - Audio-based confidence scoring
              - Phase-aware speed profile
```

### Fusion Benefits

1. **Temporal alignment:** Audio provides sub-millisecond impact timing that video cannot match. The camera-estimated speed at the audio-determined impact moment is more accurate than the camera estimate at the nearest video frame.

2. **Confidence scoring:** If audio characteristics (loudness, spectral shape) are consistent with the camera-estimated speed, confidence is higher. Disagreement suggests measurement error.

3. **Reduced computational load:** Audio-triggered recording means the vision pipeline only needs to run during actual swings, saving battery and processing time.

4. **Robustness:** If video tracking momentarily loses the club (motion blur, occlusion), audio continuity can bridge the gap and indicate whether the swing was completed.

### Research on Multi-Modal Sports Analysis

Recent work on multi-modal IoT data fusion for sports analysis shows that combining multiple sensor modalities (including audio and video) improves event detection accuracy. Approaches using spatiotemporal graph convolutional networks (ST-GCN) and Transformer architectures can mine features from heterogeneous sensor data.
Source: [ScienceDirect - Multi-modal IoT Sports Fusion](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1110016825006702)

Audio-visual integration has shown success in sports broadcast analysis, where crowd reactions and impact sounds help identify key moments. The audio stream is typically converted to mel-spectrograms for processing.
Source: [Automated Sport Highlight Detection](https://arxiv.org/html/2501.16100v2)

## Recommendations

### Phase 1: Implement Now (High Value, Low Effort)

**1. Audio-based impact detection**
- Add a lightweight audio tap alongside video recording.
- Detect the impact transient via simple amplitude threshold.
- Use the audio timestamp to identify the exact impact video frame (and interpolate sub-frame position).
- This single feature meaningfully improves speed-at-impact accuracy.

**2. Audio-based swing trigger**
- Monitor audio in low-power mode between swings.
- Detect the onset of swing sound (rising amplitude envelope) to trigger high-FPS capture.
- Reduces battery consumption and storage use.

### Phase 2: Medium-Term Enhancement

**3. Swing phase segmentation from audio**
- Build a simple state machine: IDLE -> BACKSWING -> DOWNSWING -> IMPACT -> FOLLOW_THROUGH -> IDLE.
- Train on collected swing audio data.
- Use phases to validate and improve camera-based swing detection.

**4. Strike quality feedback**
- Analyze impact sound spectrum to classify center vs. off-center strikes.
- Provide qualitative feedback alongside speed numbers.
- Requires a training dataset of labeled impacts.

### Phase 3: Exploratory / Future Research

**5. Audio-visual fusion for speed refinement**
- Explore whether combining audio features with camera data in a learned model improves speed accuracy.
- Would require significant data collection and ML development.

**6. Speed estimation from aeroacoustic analysis**
- Build a research prototype to test whether STFT-based frequency tracking of the shaft vortex shedding tone can provide useful speed estimates.
- Likely a dead end for absolute measurement but worth a small experiment.

### Not Recommended

- **Doppler-based speed measurement from passive audio** -- The physics works in theory, but the practical signal processing challenges with a broadband, non-stationary source in an uncontrolled environment make this infeasible for a consumer app.
- **Audio as primary speed measurement** -- No path to the +/-2 mph accuracy users expect from speed measurement tools.

## Summary

Audio analysis is a **valuable supplementary channel** for a camera-based golf swing speed app. Its strongest contributions are in **timing precision** (impact detection), **event segmentation** (swing phases), and **power management** (triggering recording). These applications are technically straightforward to implement on iOS using `AVAudioEngine` and `vDSP`, require minimal additional battery consumption, and meaningfully improve the quality of camera-based measurements.

Direct speed measurement from audio alone is not viable at consumer-grade accuracy levels, but audio features can serve as a useful cross-check and confidence signal for camera-based estimates.

The recommended approach is to **implement audio capture as a lightweight parallel pipeline** that runs alongside video, providing timing anchors and segmentation signals to the primary vision-based speed measurement system.
